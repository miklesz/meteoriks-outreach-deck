# Meteoriks: what juroring actually looks like

<div class="subtitle">A practical inside view</div>

???

This talk started with a simple observation: many sceners have an opinion about the Meteoriks, but the jury process is not widely understood.

This is not meant to be an official rulebook or a defence of every decision ever made. It is a practical description of the process, based on taking part in it and on conversations with other jurors and organisers.

The deck is deliberately reusable. The local examples can change, but the main questions should make sense in different communities: what does the work involve, how much time does it take, and who should consider joining?

---

# Why make this talk?

- Meteoriks results are discussed widely
- the work behind them is discussed much less
- misunderstandings fill the gaps
- potential jurors often rule themselves out too early

???

The public sees the nominees, the ceremony and the final result. Most of the work happens earlier and is much less visible.

That leaves room for two opposite myths. One is that the jury is a mysterious closed group making arbitrary choices. The other is that becoming a juror means spending every evening for months watching hundreds of productions from start to finish.

Neither picture is very useful. There is real work, real subjectivity and room for improvement, but there is also a process, useful tooling and a lot of ordinary coordination between sceners.

The point of the talk is to replace vague assumptions with a more realistic picture.

---

# Meteoriks is an open award built from category juries

- it honours productions from the previous demoscene year
- sceners can apply to become jurors
- each category has a separate jury of at least three people
- juries select nominees, then laureates and possible honourable mentions

<p class="source">Source: meteoriks.org, About and Categories</p>

???

This is the public starting point. Meteoriks describes itself as an open and inclusive award. It is not a single permanent panel choosing everything.

Each category has its own jury. That matters because the knowledge needed for oldschool productions, soundtracks, executable graphics or high-end demos is not interchangeable.

The separation also limits the workload. A juror is responsible for a smaller part of the full release landscape rather than every award category.

The names of jurors are kept anonymous until the ceremony, but applications are open. In other words, the jury is not supposed to be a fixed invitation-only club, even though direct recruiting is sometimes needed to fill specialist roles.

---

# The awards matter because attention has a long tail

<div class="pipeline four">
  <div class="step">Release</div>
  <div class="step">Nomination</div>
  <div class="step">New viewers</div>
  <div class="step">Longer memory</div>
</div>

<p class="statement">Meteoriks does not define quality, but it does influence visibility.</p>

???

Nobody needs to believe that one award can objectively rank the entire demoscene. That is not the claim.

The more modest claim is easy to observe: nominations make people revisit productions, discover things they missed and talk about them again. A nomination can give a production a second life after its original party or release weekend.

The ceremony also preserves a record of what the scene found remarkable in a particular year. Even parts of the show that are not awards, such as In Memoriam, contribute to community memory.

If the awards have that influence, then the range of people contributing their knowledge and judgement matters as well.

  <div class="card gold">

    <p>Separate juries are formed around the agreed categories.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="source">The exact category lineup may change from year to year.</p>

???

It is useful to mention this because the public process is not simply: choose a category from a permanent list and start voting.

According to the public description, accepted jurors first discuss which categories will be awarded that year. Only after that are the category juries formed.

In recent years many categories have been stable, but the lineup can still change. This also means category definitions matter. A good definition gives the jury a shared question to answer. A vague definition makes discussion drift towards personal taste or the safest common denominator.

This part of the process is one reason broad expertise helps before the actual nominations begin.

# The working process has several distinct phases

<div class="pipeline">
  <div class="step">Discovery and recommendations</div>
  <div class="step">Early screening</div>
  <div class="step">Category review</div>
  <div class="step">Nominee discussions</div>
  <div class="step">Nominee reveal</div>
  <div class="step">Laureate discussions</div>
</div>

<p class="small">The details vary between categories and jury leads.</p>

???

This is the map for the rest of the talk.

Discovery is about making sure relevant productions enter the field of view. Screening reduces a very large release list to material that needs serious category attention. Category review means watching, rewatching and comparing the plausible candidates. Then the jury has to agree on nominees.

The winner is a later decision made from that smaller nominee set. Separating these stages helps: the question "does this deserve a nomination?" is not identical to "is this the strongest nominee?"

The diagram is intentionally general. Different categories have different source material, different volumes and different ways of organising their work.

# Recommendations help discovery; they are not votes

<div class="columns">
  <div class="card">
    <h3>Common assumption</h3>
    <p>More recommendations mean better chances.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="card gold">
    <h3>What they actually do</h3>
    <p>Help the juries notice a production.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="statement"><span class="accent">Ten recommendations are not ten nomination points.</span></p>

???

This misconception comes up surprisingly often. People sometimes organise a group effort to recommend the same production, assuming that each submission increases its score.

The public site asks people to suggest productions to the juries for consideration. The useful function is discovery: make sure a strong or obscure production is not missed.

Once the production is on the radar, repeating the same suggestion should not give it extra weight. Otherwise the recommendation phase would immediately become a popularity contest and invite organised name voting.

So recommending something is useful, especially when it may be overlooked. Campaigning repeatedly for something already noticed is not the same thing.

---

# Early screening is about due diligence, not full reviews

- start from a broad release list
- use available screenshots, captures and metadata
- identify material relevant to each category
- spend serious time where it can change the shortlist

???

There is a real tension here. On one side, nobody wants to miss a worthy production simply because it was released on an obscure platform, lacked a Pouet discussion or never received a polished capture. On the other side, treating every release as if it were already a finalist would make the work impossible.

Due diligence does not mean every juror watches every production from beginning to end. It means the process has enough coverage to make a defensible judgement about what deserves closer attention.

Often a screenshot, a quick scan of a capture, the party context or prior knowledge of the release is enough to decide that something is not relevant to a particular category. The difficult cases receive more time.

---

# Category review is where expertise becomes useful

- understand what the category is trying to reward
- watch and rewatch plausible candidates
- compare reasons, not just rankings
- bring in platform or discipline context
- notice candidates other jurors may have missed

???

At this stage the work stops being a large filtering exercise and becomes a smaller, more serious comparison.

Category knowledge matters in several ways. It helps distinguish a genuinely unusual technical achievement from something familiar on that platform. It helps recognise whether a visual or musical choice is integral to the production rather than merely impressive in isolation. It also helps interpret categories that cut across platforms.

The useful discussion is not simply exchanging ordered lists. Jurors need to explain why a production fits the category and what makes it stand out. Those reasons give the group something concrete to challenge, refine or agree with.

# Nominees and laureates answer different questions

<div class="columns">
  <div class="card">
    <h3>Nominees</h3>
    <p>Which productions clearly deserve recognition in this category?</p>
  </div>
  <div class="card gold">
    <h3>Laureate</h3>
    <p>Which nominee best represents the category this year?</p>
  </div>
</div>

???

This separation is useful both practically and mentally.

The nomination phase can preserve several different kinds of excellence. The later laureate discussion has to make a harder comparison inside that already strong set.

These are group decisions, not a public popularity score and not simply an average of private rankings. How a particular jury reaches agreement can vary. Some groups resolve most questions in writing. Others need a call near the deadline. In some categories the likely winner becomes clear early; in others there is genuine disagreement until the end.

The important point for a potential juror is that the work includes discussion and coordination, not only solitary watching.

# The same process can feel very different in two juries

<div class="columns">
  <div class="card">
    <h3>Head of jury</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>organises the work</li>
      <li>keeps deadlines visible</li>
      <li>moves discussion forward</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="card gold">
    <h3>Other jurors</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>watch the material</li>
      <li>share reasons and context</li>
      <li>stay present between deadlines</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

???

This was an important point in the feedback on the first outline.

There is a common framework, but the lived experience depends heavily on people. A jury lead can create clarity by setting expectations, dividing early work, asking direct questions and making sure silence is not mistaken for agreement.

The rest of the jury matters just as much. Three names in a channel do not automatically create three active jurors. If people only appear just before the deadline, the nominal team size says little about the actual capacity.

Conversely, a small but active group can work efficiently when people keep up, explain their views and respond to each other.

---

# Conversation is part of the work, not an optional extra

- text channels are useful for links and an audit trail
- calls resolve ambiguity faster
- planned meetings work better than last-minute scheduling
- watch parties can turn work back into a scene activity

???

Several jurors described quiet channels as one of the least satisfying parts of the experience. Watching productions alone and dropping occasional rankings into Slack is not much of a jury process.

Calls can help at three natural points: a short kickoff, the nomination deadline and the final winner discussion. They do not all need to happen if the group has already reached agreement, but planning them in advance avoids the usual scheduling failure.

An in-person watch party is even better when geography allows it. Apart from making discussion easier, it changes the mood. Watching demos together on a large screen feels like a demoscene activity rather than administrative work.

# The workload is real, but it is not one fixed number

<div class="columns">
  <div>
    <h3>It grows with</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>category breadth</li>
      <li>release volume</li>
      <li>missing captures or metadata</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>It shrinks with</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>experience and prior knowledge</li>
      <li>good screening tools</li>
      <li>an active jury</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="statement">The 2026 screening list contained almost 700 productions.</p>

???

The number is useful for scale, but it needs context. One local screening report for the 2026 process contains 694 productions. That does not mean each juror was expected to watch all 694 in full.

Some categories naturally cover a narrow, easy-to-identify set. Others, such as concept or technical achievement, can potentially draw candidates from almost anywhere. A juror taking two broad categories will have a different experience from someone covering one compact category.

The timing also matters. Following releases during the year spreads the work and leaves useful memory. Starting from zero shortly before the deadline makes exactly the same list feel much larger.

---

# A few habits make the work much more manageable

- follow major party releases during the year
- use screenshots and video captures to triage
- keep links and short notes while watching
- ask for help with unfamiliar platforms
- do not postpone all discussion until the deadline

???

None of these tricks eliminates the responsibility, but together they change the scale of the task.

If you already watched a production at a party, a title and screenshot may be enough to restore the context. If a capture exists, scanning it can tell you whether the production needs a full rewatch. Browser tooling can reduce friction when moving between Demozoo, captures and platform information.

The hardest items are often those without screenshots, captures or clear metadata, especially on obscure hardware. This is also why community recommendations and people with specialised knowledge remain useful.

---

# Active experts may also have work in contention

- the most useful jurors are often active sceners
- active participation can create conflicts of interest
- excluding every active maker would also exclude expertise
- clear rules and visible handling matter for trust

???

This is a real issue, and it should not be waved away.

From inside a jury, a conflict may be handled correctly: the affected person does not vote, does not lead the discussion and only answers factual questions when asked. From outside, people may still see a juror's production among the nominees and assume the worst because they cannot see that handling.

The opposite policy - nobody with a potentially competitive production may serve - sounds clean but can remove exactly the people with current platform or discipline knowledge. There is no magical solution here.

Before this deck is treated as official outreach material, the exact wording of this slide and the current conflict-of-interest policy should be confirmed by Meteoriks.

---

# More good jurors improve more than workload

- broader expertise and fewer blind spots
- more resilient teams when somebody gets busy
- better discussion instead of isolated rankings
- less dependence on last-minute recruiting
- more confidence in the result from outside

???

The benefit is not linear task division. Doubling the jury does not simply halve every hour, because everyone still needs enough shared knowledge to discuss the shortlist.

But additional engaged jurors help in several other ways. They increase the chance that someone understands a platform, remembers an overlooked release or challenges a weak consensus. They also make the group less fragile when real life interrupts one person's availability.

This is why the goal is not merely more names. It is more people who will actually take part throughout the process.

---

# Who should consider becoming a juror?

- you follow at least one area of the scene with real interest
- you are willing to watch, compare and explain
- you can stay engaged over a period of time
- you can disagree without turning it personal
- you care about recognising other people's work

<p class="statement">You do not need to know everything or be scene-famous.</p>

???

People who would be good jurors often do not apply because they do not consider themselves authoritative enough.

The useful threshold is not celebrity and not encyclopaedic knowledge of every platform. It is enough expertise to contribute meaningfully in a category, plus reliability and willingness to discuss.

At the same time, this is not a zero-commitment honorary role. Somebody who wants only to appear in the jury list, or to cast a ranking three hours before the deadline, will not solve the capacity problem.

---

# Recruitment has to leave the usual scene bubble

- the busiest public channels do not reach everybody
- local communities and language groups have their own spaces
- platform and party communities know their specialists
- personal invitations often reach people who would never self-nominate

???

Posting one announcement in a large Discord server can create the illusion that the whole scene has been reached. In practice many people mute busy servers and only follow smaller, more focused spaces.

Useful outreach can happen through local scenes, platform communities, party organisers, group servers, social media and direct conversation. People inside those communities know who follows releases, who has specialist knowledge and who may simply need encouragement to apply.

This is the reason for making the deck reusable. A person visiting a party or community should not have to invent the entire explanation from scratch.

---

# The process can improve without pretending it is broken

- explain recommendations more clearly
- recruit earlier and through more channels
- make calls and watch parties easier to organise
- clarify what enters each category's review set
- keep asking jurors what made the experience good or bad

???

The constructive position is not "everything is fine" and not "the awards are hopeless".

Some improvements are mostly communication: make the recommendation mechanism clearer and show the broad phases of the process. Others are organisational: recruit earlier, plan calls and make sure the workload is visible before deadlines become urgent.

Preselection transparency is a larger policy question. Showing which productions are being considered for which categories could reduce omissions and improve trust, but it needs a deliberate decision from the organisers.

The most important habit may simply be treating juror feedback as process data. If somebody leaves saying the experience was not enjoyable, finding out why is more useful than assuming they were not a good fit.

The process is not perfect, but it matters. It gets better when more good people take part and when the people already involved keep improving how the work is organised.

Questions are welcome, including sceptical ones. The goal of the talk is not to remove disagreement, but to make it better informed.
